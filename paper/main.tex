% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================================

\begin{abstract}
Accuracy treats every LLM error as equally costly. The actuarial
frequency-severity framework of \citet{beauchemin2026position}
fixes this in theory but was demonstrated on a single
$82$-query insurance corpus. We provide the first multi-domain
empirical validation: $12$ open-weight LLMs ($8$\,B--$120$\,B
parameters) on $11$ benchmarks across finance, medicine, law,
and insurance, with severity labels derived from each corpus's
structured fields. At full-corpus scale, accuracy and $\ES$
rankings agree strongly in every domain (Kendall
$\tau \in [0.58, 1.00]$), overturning the small-sample
divergence claim. The framework's residual value lies in
$72$ dollar-magnitude inversions, a variance decomposition whose
severity share spans $1\%$ (medical) to $67\%$ (finance), and a
routing tradeoff that cuts $\ES$ by $84$--$99\%$ at $18$--$53\%$
human-review deferral. We release \seveval{}, a HuggingFace-style
Python library, alongside the annotations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Accuracy treats every error as equally costly: a misclassified
spam email and a misclassified cancer diagnosis count the same.
As LLMs move into high-stakes deployments, that assumption
breaks. A revenue answer of \$11.6\,B instead of \$14.2\,B
shifts a trading decision; a missed sepsis cue delays
intervention; mis-extracting an indemnity clause changes a
contract's risk profile. The per-error cost of these failures
spans orders of magnitude, yet accuracy assigns them all the
same penalty.

\citet{beauchemin2026position} proposed adopting actuarial
frequency-severity models from insurance mathematics to address
this. Their framework decomposes operational risk into a
\emph{frequency} component (error rate $p$) and a \emph{severity}
component (a distribution $\bpi$ over cost levels), yielding a
compound loss $S = \sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i$ whose expected value, tail
quantiles and reserves are computable in closed form or by Monte
Carlo. That work was conceptual: feasibility on a single
$82$-query insurance corpus, no empirical validation across
domains or models.

This paper provides that validation. We evaluate $12$ open-weight
LLMs (granite-3.2-8b through gpt-oss-120b, dense, MoE and
reasoning-tuned) on $11$ benchmarks across four high-stakes
domains, with severity labels derived from each corpus's
structured fields. Each (model, dataset) cell uses the
\emph{full} test corpus, totalling $\sim$$149$\,K predictions over
$96$ cells. We do not aim to find the best model; we aim to
characterise the cost picture the framework surfaces, and where
it adds signal beyond accuracy.

Our contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item At full-corpus scale, accuracy and $\ES$ rankings agree
    strongly in every domain (Kendall
    $\tau \in [0.58, 1.00]$, all $p < 0.01$). The
    \enquote{accuracy~$\neq$~economic-risk} claim of the conceptual
    paper does not survive a saturated corpus.
  \item Where rankings agree, dollar magnitudes still diverge.
    We detect $72$ strict inversions $\mathrm{acc}(A) >
    \mathrm{acc}(B)$ and $\ES(A) > \ES(B)$; the largest is
    DeepSeek-R1-70B vs.\ Llama-3.3-70B on JudgeBERT, $+0.7$
    accuracy points for $+\$28.7$\,M per $10{,}000$ outputs.
  \item The variance decomposition of $\ES$ into severity vs.\
    frequency components varies from $1\%$ (medical) to $67\%$
    (finance) across domains. Reducing $\ES$ requires changing
    \emph{which} errors a model makes in finance / legal, and
    changing \emph{how often} it errs in medical / insurance.
  \item HITL routing cuts $\ES$ by $85\%$ in finance at only
    $18\%$ deferral (cheap-routing regime) and by $98$--$99\%$
    in the other three domains at $\sim 50\%$ deferral
    (deep-routing regime).
  \item We release \seveval{}, a HuggingFace-style Python library
    (\autoref{sec:app-library-overview}), the rule-based
    severity tables (\autoref{sec:severity-annotation}), and the
    cost-calibration evidence
    (\autoref{sec:app-calibration}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics and cost-sensitive learning.}
Accuracy ignores error heterogeneity. NLP metrics that partly
address this (BLEU \citep{papineni2002bleu}, ROUGE
\citep{lin2004rouge}, BERTScore \citep{zhang2020bertscore},
BLEURT \citep{sellam2020bleurt}) measure linguistic quality, not
operational risk; MQM \citep{lommel2014mqm} weights minor/major/
critical errors at $1/5/25$, the closest NLP precedent. Cost-sensitive
learning \citep{elkan2001foundations,drummond2006cost,ling2008cost}
optimises models under a known cost matrix at training time but
is restricted to classification with fixed classes. None of these
maps errors to dollar-denominated organisational losses.

\paragraph{Risk-aware AI evaluation and actuarial loss models.}
Regulatory frameworks (EU AI Act \citep{euaiact2024}, NIST AI RMF
\citep{nist2023rmf}, ISO/IEC~42001 \citep{iso42001}) prescribe
risk tiering without quantitative loss measures. Risk taxonomies
\citep{weidinger2022taxonomy,bergman2024stela,kirk2024personalising}
and ontologies \citep{golpayegani2022airo} formalise harms
qualitatively. The Basel~II/III operational-risk Loss
Distribution Approach \citep{basel2006} and its cyber-risk
extensions \citep{malavasi2022cyber,eling2016cyber} use the same
frequency-severity decomposition we adopt; recent Federal Reserve
work \citep{bosfed2025ai} applies it to AI-related operational
losses. \citet{beauchemin2026position} bridge actuarial methods
and AI evaluation; our paper is the empirical companion.

\paragraph{HITL deferral and large-scale LLM benchmarks.}
Routing uncertain predictions to a human has been studied under
\emph{learning to defer}
\citep{madras2018predict,mozannar2020consistent}, \emph{selective
prediction} \citep{elyaniv2010selective,geifman2017selective}, and
post-hoc deferral estimators
\citep{narasimhan2022posthoc,okati2021differentiable}; we recast
it as excess-of-loss reinsurance. Large-scale LLM evaluation
suites (HELM \citep{liang2023helm}, MMLU
\citep{hendrycks2021mmlu}, Open LLM Leaderboard
\citep{beeching2023openllm}, Chatbot Arena
\citep{chiang2024chatbot}) and domain-specific benchmarks
\citep{islam2023financebench,chen2021finqa,jin2021medqa,kazi2024medcalc,hendrycks2021cuad,koreeda2021contractnli}
all report accuracy-class metrics. Our work augments these
benchmarks with a per-instance severity label and the actuarial
risk measures that follow.

\section{Actuarial Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

We recapitulate the framework of \citet{beauchemin2026position};
formal derivations are in the original paper.

\subsection{Frequency-Severity Decomposition}

We follow the standard collective-risk model of actuarial science
\citep{klugman2012loss}: an AI system produces $n$ outputs per
evaluation period, each correct (probability $1-p$) or erroneous
(probability $p$). Each error falls into one of $K$ severity
categories with costs $c_1 < \cdots < c_K$ and probabilities
$\bpi = (\pi_1, \ldots, \pi_K)$:
\begin{align}
  N &\sim \mathrm{Bin}(n, p), \label{eq:frequency} \\
  X_i &\in \{c_1, \ldots, c_K\}, \quad \mathbb{P}(X_i = c_k) = \pi_k. \label{eq:severity}
\end{align}

\subsection{Compound Loss}

The aggregate loss $S = \sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i$ has closed-form moments:
\begin{align}
  \ES &= n p \, \mu_X, &
  \mathrm{Var}(S) &= n p \, \sigma_X^2 + n p (1\!-\!p) \, \mu_X^2,
  \label{eq:expected_loss}
\end{align}
where $\mu_X = \sum_k c_k \pi_k$ and
$\sigma_X^2 = \sum_k c_k^2 \pi_k - \mu_X^2$. The expected loss
$\ES$ captures both error frequency ($p$) and economic impact
($\mu_X$): two systems with identical accuracy can differ arbitrarily
in $\ES$ if $\bpi_A \neq \bpi_B$.

\subsection{Risk Measures}

We compute the distribution of $S$ via Monte Carlo simulation
($10^5$ replications) and extract two tail risk measures: the
\textbf{Value-at-Risk}
$\VaR_\alpha(S) = \inf\{s : \mathbb{P}(S \leq s) \geq \alpha\}$,
i.e.\ the loss exceeded with probability $1 - \alpha$; and the
\textbf{Tail Value-at-Risk}
$\TVaR_\alpha(S) = \mathbb{E}[S \mid S > \VaR_\alpha(S)]$,
the expected loss in the worst $(1 - \alpha)$ scenarios.

\subsection{Ruin Theory and Reserves}

For multi-period deployment, the surplus process
$U_t = u + nrt - \sum_{s=1}^{t} S_s$ tracks the remaining risk
budget, where $u$ is the initial reserve and $r$ is the net revenue
per output. The ruin probability
$\psi(u) = \mathbb{P}(\exists\, t \geq 1 : U_t < 0)$ is bounded
by Lundberg's inequality $\psi(u) \leq e^{-Ru}$, where $R$ is the
adjustment coefficient obtained by solving
$\lambda(M_X(R) - 1) = nrR$ with $\lambda = np$
\citep{dickson2016insurance}. This yields a minimum reserve
$u^* = -\ln(\psi^*) / R$ for a target ruin tolerance $\psi^*$.

\subsection{Human-in-the-Loop Routing}

Following the excess-of-loss reinsurance analogy
\citep{beauchemin2026position}, outputs with estimated severity above
a retention threshold $d$ are routed to human review at cost $h$.
The routing ratio $\rho = \sum_{k : c_k > d} \pi_k$ gives the
fraction redirected, yielding total expected cost:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}[C] = n p (1 - \rho) \, \mu_X^{\mathrm{ret}} + n p \rho \, h,
  \label{eq:routing}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_X^{\mathrm{ret}}$ is the mean severity of retained errors.

The calibration of each cost level draws on multiple sources:
regulatory penalties (SEC SAB~99, SOX~\S906, FINRA, FDA, EU~AI~Act),

settlements), operational loss databases (ORX for banking, IBM/Ponemon
for data breaches), and published case studies of AI failures in
production \citep{aircanada2024,prosperspark2023}. Detailed
calibration evidence is provided in \autoref{sec:app-calibration}.

\paragraph{Severity assignment.}
We do not collect new human annotations. For each dataset, we
define deterministic rules that map an existing structured field
of the corpus (clause type, deal-point category, fixed
hypothesis, academic specialty, etc.) to a severity tier. The
tier reflects the cost of \emph{erring on that instance}, not a
property of the question: a CUAD extraction is labelled critical
when missing the clause exposes the buyer to material legal
risk, not because the clause text is verbose. The rule-based
approach is reproducible and removes per-instance annotator
subjectivity. We validate each rule system against an
independent LLM annotator (Claude Sonnet~4) using the severity
rubric as a one-shot prompt, reporting Cohen's $\kappa$ \citep{cohen1960} and
adjacent agreement
($\pm 1$ level).

\subsection{Models}
\label{sec:models}

We evaluate $12$ open-weight checkpoints chosen to span the
deployed LLM design space: $8$\,B to $120$\,B parameters, dense
and mixture-of-experts, base and reasoning-tuned, FP16, FP8 and
MXFP4 quantizations. Concretely: Granite-3.2-8B, Phi-4,
Gemma-3-12B/27B, Qwen3-14B/30B-A3B, QwQ-32B, GPT-OSS-20B/120B,
Qwen2.5-72B, Llama-3.3-70B and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-70B. All
inference runs on a $3\times$RTX~6000~Ada workstation via vLLM.
The exact checkpoint identifiers, quantization details, and the
inference hardware configuration are in
\autoref{sec:app-models} and \autoref{sec:app-hardware}.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

Each dataset is evaluated under an \emph{original} prompt
(reproducing the dataset's published protocol) and a
\emph{standard} HELM-style prompt \citep{liang2023helm} as an
ablation. Predictions are scored by a cascade (MCQ letter, yes/no,
numeric at $5\%$ tolerance, exact, fuzzy). For each (model,
dataset) pair we compute $\hat{p}{=}1{-}\mathrm{acc}$, the
severity profile $\hat{\bpi}$, and the actuarial measures
$\widehat{\ES}$, $\VaR_{0.95}$, $\TVaR_{0.95}$, $R$, $u^\ast$ via
$10^5$ Monte Carlo replications at $n{=}10{,}000$ outputs. Wilson
score intervals on accuracy, Goodman simultaneous intervals on
$\hat{\bpi}$, and percentile bootstrap intervals on the actuarial
measures are computed at $\alpha{=}0.95$ throughout. Full
methodological detail (scoring cascade, CI formulas, paired
bootstrap, per-domain aggregation) is in
\autoref{sec:app-protocol}; the cost-calibration robustness check
is in \autoref{sec:app-robustness}. The analysis is organised
around three research questions:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt,labelindent=0pt]
  \item[RQ1 (Ranking divergence and inversions).] How closely aligned
    are model rankings by accuracy and by $\ES$ within a domain, and
    do concrete pairs invert? We report Kendall $\tau$
    \citep{kendall1938} between the
    two rank orders together with its $p$-value, plus the count and
    dollar magnitude of strict inversions where
    $\mathrm{acc}(A) > \mathrm{acc}(B)$ but $\ES(A) > \ES(B)$.
  \item[RQ2 (Severity vs.\ frequency variance).] How much of the
    inter-model variance in $\ES$ is attributable to the severity
    profile $\bpi$ versus the error rate $p$? We report a variance
    decomposition that holds one factor at the domain mean while
    letting the other vary.
  \item[RQ3 (Routing benefit).] Does the cost reduction from
    human-in-the-loop routing covary with the tail heaviness of the
    domain's cost distribution? We report the per-domain mean cost
    reduction and the Spearman correlation with the coefficient of
    variation of $\mathbf{c}$.
\end{description}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We report results on the $96$ (model, dataset) cells, each
evaluated on the \emph{full} test corpus. All risk measures are
computed with $n{=}10{,}000$ outputs per evaluation period,
$20{,}000$ Monte Carlo replications, and $\alpha{=}0.95$. The
complete per-pair table is provided in
\autoref{sec:app-full-results}. The headline numbers below
come from $12$ open-weight local models on $8$ benchmarks, with
per-cell sample sizes ranging from $150$ (FinanceBench) to
$39{,}231$ (MAUD).

\subsection{RQ1: Ranking alignment and dollar-magnitude inversions}
\label{sec:rq1}

\paragraph{Rankings agree, contrary to the small-sample claim.}
At full-corpus scale, accuracy and $\ES$ rankings are strongly
correlated in every domain: per-domain Kendall $\tau$ is
$0.58$ in finance ($p{=}0.009$), $0.64$ in insurance ($p{=}0.003$),
$0.97$ in legal ($p{=}5{\times}10^{-8}$), and $1.00$ in medical
($p{=}4{\times}10^{-9}$) (\autoref{tab:h1}). The dramatic
divergence reported by \citet{beauchemin2026position} on an $82$-query
insurance corpus, and the $\tau \approx 0.4$ we ourselves obtained
on a $1$\,K-per-dataset pilot run, do not reproduce once the
corpora are saturated: medical and legal collapse to
near-monotone ranking, finance and insurance retain moderate but
significant correlation. This is itself a contribution: \emph{the
case for severity-aware evaluation cannot rest on rerankings
disagreeing}, because at scale they largely do not.

\paragraph{Dollar magnitudes are still missing from accuracy.}
The framework's residual value is what an accuracy-only
leaderboard cannot show: the cost of the rank that is correct.
We detect $72$ strict accuracy-vs-$\ES$ inversions where
$\mathrm{acc}(A) > \mathrm{acc}(B)$ \emph{and} $\ES(A) > \ES(B)$.
The largest is on JudgeBERT: DeepSeek-R1-70B edges Llama-3.3-70B
by $+0.7$ accuracy points yet costs $\$28.7$\,M more per
$10{,}000$ outputs. On TAT-QA, qwen2.5-72b is $+0.2$ points
over DeepSeek-R1-70B for $+\$8.7$\,M; on FinanceBench,
granite-3.2-8b is $+9.3$ over DeepSeek-R1-70B yet costs
$\$3.86$\,M more. The most visible \emph{rank flip} is in
finance: DeepSeek-R1-70B is rank $9$ by accuracy and rank $1$
by $\ES$, a $+8$-rank rise driven by a severity profile
concentrated on negligible/minor levels
(\autoref{fig:ranking_finance}). The takeaway is no longer
\emph{\enquote{accuracy gets the leaderboard wrong}}; it is
\emph{\enquote{accuracy doesn't tell you how much being wrong costs}}.

\subsection{RQ2: Severity vs.\ frequency variance}
\label{sec:rq2}

Where the framework adds the most signal is the decomposition of
inter-model $\sigma^2_{\ES}$ into a severity-only
($\sigma^2_{\bpi}$) and a frequency-only ($\sigma^2_p$) component.
Across the four domains the severity share spans nearly the full
$[0, 1]$ interval: $0.67$ in finance, $0.46$ in legal, $0.23$ in
insurance, $0.01$ in medical (\autoref{fig:variance}). The
spread itself is the result. In finance, models differ from one
another mostly in \emph{which} errors they make: identical
accuracy can hide a $2{\times}$ dollar exposure depending on the
severity profile. In medical, models differ almost entirely in
\emph{how often} they err: the severity profiles are nearly
indistinguishable across the $12$ models, so error rate
\emph{is} a faithful rank. Insurance and legal lie between, with
frequency carrying the larger share. The practical implication:
the lever that most reduces $\ES$ is domain-specific. In finance,
the marginal dollar is spent improving severity selection (avoid
the worst errors); in medical, it is spent improving accuracy
outright. The decomposition is sensitive to the cost vector and
to the model mix; we report it as descriptive rather than
confirmatory, but the cross-domain spread is robust to a
$\pm 20\%$ perturbation of every cost level
(\autoref{sec:rq3}, robustness check).

\subsection{RQ3: Routing tradeoff}
\label{sec:rq3}

The routing-as-reinsurance analysis turns the framework into an
operational decision tool: given a retention threshold $d{=}c_3$
(defer \enquote{major} and \enquote{critical} errors) and a human-review cost
$h{=}\$50$, how much $\ES$ is removed per fraction of queries
deferred? The answer is domain-stratified
(\autoref{tab:h4}, \autoref{fig:routing}). Finance is the
\enquote{cheap routing} regime: deferring the median $18\%$ of queries
removes $84.60\%$ of $\ES$ -- a leverage ratio of $4.7{:}1$. The
three other domains live in a \enquote{deep routing} regime, requiring
deferral of roughly half the queries ($49$--$53\%$) but extracting
near-total reductions ($98$--$99\%$). Insurance is the steepest
combination ($99\%$ at $49\%$ routing), consistent with its
heavy-tailed cost vector (CV $= 1.63$) absorbing the deferred
tail. The pattern is robust to a $\pm 20\%$ multiplicative
perturbation of every cost level (\autoref{sec:app-robustness}):
the minimum Spearman correlation of per-model rankings under
perturbation is $0.90$ in finance and $1.00$ in the other three
domains.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The framework, not the model, is what we are evaluating}

The $12$ open-weight checkpoints we benchmark span $8$\,B to
$120$\,B parameters, dense and MoE, base and reasoning-tuned,
FP16 and FP8 and MXFP4. They are not interchangeable on
accuracy, $\ES$, or $\bpi$, yet the three framework outputs
surface the same kind of signal regardless of which model we
look at: a dollar-magnitude inversion in RQ1 that an accuracy
leaderboard cannot expose, a domain-specific severity-vs-
frequency variance share in RQ2, and a routing tradeoff in RQ3
that converts the framework's outputs into an operating point
(\enquote{cheap} or \enquote{deep} routing). The contribution is therefore
not \enquote{model $X$ is best} but \enquote{the framework reveals
decision-relevant cost structure that generalises across the
model design space we tested}. This is the empirical
complement to the conceptual demonstration of
\citet{beauchemin2026position}, which showed feasibility on a
single $82$-query insurance corpus with one model.

\subsection{What the actuarial framework adds over accuracy}

The compound loss model produces three families of quantities that
accuracy alone does not provide. The expected loss $\ES$ summarises
the per-period economic exposure under a given $\mathbf{c}$; the
tail measures $\VaR_{0.95}$ and $\TVaR_{0.95}$ characterise the
loss in adverse scenarios; the Lundberg reserve $u^\ast$ translates
a target ruin tolerance into a dollar budget. These outputs are
expressed in the same units that risk-management functions
(Basel~II/III, SOX, COSO) already use, which can lower the
translation cost between AI evaluation and enterprise risk
processes. The actuarial decomposition therefore complements
accuracy rather than replaces it: the error rate $p$ remains a
primary input, but $\bpi$ and $\mathbf{c}$ are needed to recover
the economic picture.

\subsection{When does severity matter most?}

The variance decomposition for RQ2 is the most empirically novel
result. The mechanism is intuitive: $\ES = n\, p\, \mu_X$, and
$\mu_X$ spans three orders of magnitude across severity levels in
all four of our taxonomies, so two systems with the same $p$ but
different $\bpi$ produce $\ES$ values that can differ by an order
of magnitude. The actual share carried by $\sigma^2_{\bpi}$
\emph{across} our $12$ models, however, varies dramatically by
domain: $0.67$ in finance, $0.46$ in legal, $0.23$ in insurance,
$0.01$ in medical. The medical near-zero is itself informative:
the $12$ models cluster on essentially the same severity profile
on HEAD-QA, so what separates them is purely $p$. Finance is the
opposite -- error rates are tightly clustered around $35$--$40\%$
but $\bpi$ ranges from concentrated on negligible
(DeepSeek-R1-70B) to spread over major/critical (Granite-3.2-8B).
Accuracy collapses both regimes onto a single rank.

\subsection{Human-in-the-loop routing}

Modelling deferral as excess-of-loss reinsurance gives a closed-form
breakeven cost for human review, $h^\ast = \mu_X^{\text{route}}$,
the mean severity of the routed errors. In our snapshot, even a
$\$50$ human-review cost is well below this breakeven for medical
and insurance taxonomies, leading to large estimated cost
reductions when \enquote{major} and \enquote{critical} errors are routed
($d=c_3$). We caution that the per-error severity used in routing
is a function of $\bpi$ estimated from observed errors, not of an
oracle severity classifier; the realised benefit depends on how
accurately routing decisions are made at inference time.

\subsection{Limitations of cost calibration}

Cost vectors are approximate. Real costs depend on context,
downstream action, detection probability, and whether an error
propagates. Our levels represent expected per-instance costs
conditioned on an undetected error. The robustness analysis (\autoref{sec:app-robustness})
suggests that $\pm 20\%$ perturbations do not overturn rankings in
the snapshot we report, but practitioners should recalibrate
$\mathbf{c}$ to their organization's loss data when available.

\subsection{Comparison with MQM scoring}

The closest precedent in NLP is the MQM weighting scheme
\citep{lommel2014mqm}, with weights $\{1, 5, 25\}$ for minor,
major and critical errors. Our framework differs in three ways.
First, costs are calibrated against external evidence (regulatory
penalties, litigation data, operational loss reports) rather than
chosen by convention. Second, we report tail measures (VaR, TVaR)
and reserves in addition to a weighted mean. Third, the routing
analysis quantifies the cost--benefit trade-off of human review,
which MQM does not address.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We applied the actuarial frequency-severity framework of
\citet{beauchemin2026position} to a multi-model, multi-domain LLM
evaluation. The framework decomposes operational risk into the error
rate and the severity profile, and yields three families of measures
(expected loss, tail risk, reserves) that complement accuracy.

Across $12$ open-weight models and four high-stakes domains
evaluated at full corpus, accuracy and $\ES$ rankings are
strongly correlated everywhere (Kendall $\tau$ from $0.58$ to
$1.00$): the dramatic divergence reported on a single $82$-query
insurance pilot is a small-sample artefact, not a stable empirical
regularity. The framework's residual value lies elsewhere. It
surfaces $72$ dollar-magnitude inversions in which the
higher-accuracy model is the more expensive one; it decomposes
$\sigma^2_{\ES}$ into severity ($0.01$--$0.67$) and frequency
components that vary by domain, revealing whether the lever for
reducing $\ES$ is \enquote{which errors} or \enquote{how often}; and it
yields a routing tradeoff that cuts $\ES$ by $85$\,\%
(finance, at $18$\,\% routing) up to $99$\,\% (insurance, medical,
at $\sim 50$\,\% routing). The pattern holds across the
heterogeneous model sample, supporting the claim that the
framework, not any single model choice, exposes the cost
structure that accuracy hides. Extending to the three new
corpora queued for the camera-ready (MedMCQA, DDI 2013,
PrivacyQA), and to additional severity taxonomies is the
natural next step; the released \seveval{}
library and \texttt{run\_full\_pipeline.sh} script make that
extension reproducible. We release the code, severity
annotations, and analysis scripts to support adoption of
severity-aware evaluation as a complement to accuracy in
high-stakes deployments.

\section*{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Cost calibration.}
Our cost vectors are grounded in regulatory and market evidence
but the mapping from severity tiers to dollar amounts is
approximate; organisations within a domain face different cost
structures by size and regulatory exposure. The robustness
analysis (\autoref{sec:app-robustness}) shows rankings are
stable under $\pm 20\%$ perturbation, but practitioners should
recalibrate to their context.

\paragraph{Binary correctness.}
Each output is classified as correct or erroneous, discarding
partial correctness: \$$14.0$\,B instead of \$$14.2$\,B is
\enquote{wrong} but far less consequential than \$$11.6$\,B.
Continuous severity scoring is left to future work.

\paragraph{Severity-rule provenance.}
Severity is derived from a structured field of the source corpus
(clause type, deal-point category, fixed hypothesis, academic
specialty, OPP-115 category, DDI relation type). For the three
finance benchmarks the field does not exist natively and we use
a defensible answer-type$\times$metric-type matrix; an
independent LLM annotator validates with adjacent agreement
$94\%$, $\kappa{=}0.46$. Three benchmarks initially considered
(MedQA, MedCalc-Bench, RAG~Insurance) were dropped because their
keyword-based labels could not be defended at the
critical-vs-major boundary.

\paragraph{Single-corpus domains.}
Medical and insurance contain a single fully-evaluated benchmark
each (HEAD-QA and JudgeBERT); domain-level results there
($\tau$=$1.00$ and $0.64$) reflect within-corpus agreement, not
generalisation across the domain. The three new corpora
(MedMCQA, DDI~2013, PrivacyQA) on the dataset table will address
this once their full-corpus results land.

\paragraph{Inversion rate.}
The $72$ dollar-magnitude inversions arise from $528$ ordered
within-domain model pairs ($\binom{12}{2}$ pairs across $8$
datasets), so $14\%$ of all comparisons or $27\%$ of those with
positive accuracy gap. The framework surfaces a meaningful
minority of mispriced pairs, not a leaderboard reshuffle.

\paragraph{Pilot vs.\ full corpus is not a controlled ablation.}
The $\tau \approx 0.4$ we obtained on a $1$\,K-per-dataset pilot
also used the three later-dropped corpora and the broken
\texttt{mistral-small-3} checkpoint. The shift to $\tau \in
[0.6, 1.0]$ at full corpus therefore mixes saturation with the
cleaner roster; we cannot separate the two contributions without
a controlled study.

\paragraph{Scope.}
We evaluate English-language benchmarks across four domains.
The framework is domain-agnostic but the cost calibrations and
severity taxonomies do not transfer directly to other domains
(e.g., code generation, content moderation) without
domain-expert input. The evaluation is a single-point-in-time
snapshot; capabilities drift, and continuous monitoring via
\seveval{} is needed in production.

\section*{Ethics Statement}
\label{sec:ethics}

This work evaluates existing language models on publicly available
benchmarks and does not involve human subjects research. The severity
annotations assign economic cost estimates to error categories but do
not make normative claims about the relative value of human outcomes
(e.g., we do not equate a medical error with a financial error based
on dollar amounts). The cost calibrations are derived from publicly
available regulatory and market data and are intended as illustrative
defaults for risk budgeting, not as authoritative valuations of human
welfare.

We release all code, annotations, and evaluation results to support
reproducibility. The \seveval{} library is released under an open-source
license. We note that the severity framework, like any evaluation
metric, can be used to inform but not replace human judgment in
deployment decisions.

\section{The \seveval{} Library}
\label{sec:app-library-overview}

We release \seveval{}, an open-source Python library implementing
the actuarial evaluation pipeline. The library follows three design
choices : (1)~a HuggingFace-style \texttt{evaluate.Metric} subclass
(\texttt{CompoundLossMetric}) so the risk measures plug into existing
evaluation pipelines alongside accuracy; (2)~minimal core
dependencies (NumPy, SciPy), with pandas and matplotlib loaded
lazily for DataFrame export and plotting; (3)~domain-agnostic
design, with built-in taxonomies for finance, medicine, law and
insurance plus a registration API for custom domains. Six modules
cover compound loss simulation, risk measures, ruin theory, routing
analysis, severity taxonomies, and sensitivity analysis; usage
examples are in \autoref{sec:app-library}.

\section{Severity Annotation Details by Dataset}
\label{sec:app-severity-by-dataset}

We detail the deterministic mapping rules and one representative
instance per dataset. Each rule maps a \emph{structured} field
of the source corpus to a four-level tier; mappings never
re-interpret raw question text.

\paragraph{FinanceBench.}
Severity comes from cross-tabulating \emph{answer type} (dollar
amount in millions/billions, percentage, ratio, yes/no,
free-text) with \emph{metric type} (core financial statement,
operational, derived, descriptive). The gold answer to \enquote{Does
Corning have positive working capital based on FY2022 data?}
is \$$831$M positive; mis-stating the sign of a yes/no over a
core operational metric can flip a credit decision
(\emph{critical}). Mis-reporting Amazon's FY2017 DPO of
$93.86$ (a derived ratio over a non-core operational metric)
typically yields a smaller business consequence (\emph{minor}).

\paragraph{FinQA and TAT-QA.}
Same two-axis matrix as FinanceBench. Mis-answering \enquote{by what
percentage did average borrowings decrease from $2016$ to
$2017$?} (gold $-8.40\%$, FinQA) is a small-magnitude reasoning
slip on a derived ratio (\emph{minor}); mis-answering \enquote{What
does the deferred income taxes reflect?} (free-form span,
TAT-QA) yields a descriptive paraphrase error that does not
change a downstream accounting decision (\emph{negligible}).

\paragraph{HEAD-QA.}
Severity follows the dataset's six \emph{academic-category}
labels by clinical-decision impact: pharmacology
$\to$ critical, medicine and nursing $\to$ major, psychology
$\to$ minor, biology and chemistry $\to$ negligible. A wrong
answer on a pharmacology stem defining \enquote{Parameter} propagates
into every downstream biostatistics decision
(\emph{critical}); a wrong answer on a biology fact-recall
stem (e.g.\ \enquote{does carotid body stimulation decrease arterial
PCO\textsubscript{2}?}) carries little clinical-decision
exposure (\emph{negligible}).

\paragraph{CUAD.}
The dataset's $41$ clause types map to severity by contractual
risk, following due-diligence practice
\citep{guha2023legalbench}: indemnification and liability
limitation $\to$ critical, non-compete and IP assignment
$\to$ major, renewal and notice $\to$ minor, definitional
$\to$ negligible. Failing to extract a \enquote{Termination for
Convenience} clause exposes the buyer to unilateral
cancellation (\emph{critical}); failing on an \enquote{Audit Rights}
clause may delay compliance review but is rarely catastrophic
(\emph{major}).

\paragraph{MAUD and ContractNLI.}
Both reuse the CUAD clause-type risk schedule. An incorrect MAUD
answer on whether the target's securities are subject to a
\enquote{disproportionate impact} modifier in the Material Adverse
Effect definition directly mis-states a deal closing condition
(\emph{critical}). An incorrect ContractNLI label on the
hypothesis \enquote{Receiving Party may independently develop
information similar to Confidential Information} generally
turns one boilerplate provision into another, so the business
exposure of the error is small (\emph{negligible}).

\paragraph{JudgeBERT.}
Severity is the cross product of the simplified clause's legal
category (exclusion, indemnity, definition, \dots) and the
human-evaluator score on meaning preservation: clauses bearing
material legal weight (exclusions, consequences of
non-compliance) at low evaluator score $\to$ critical;
definitional clauses at high score $\to$ negligible.

\paragraph{MedMCQA, DDI~2013, PrivacyQA (queued).}
\textbf{MedMCQA} reuses HEAD-QA's specialty mapping on the
\texttt{subject\_name} field. \textbf{DDI~2013} maps the native
relation type to severity directly: \emph{effect} $\to$
critical, \emph{advice}/\emph{mechanism} $\to$ major,
\emph{int} $\to$ minor, \emph{false} (no interaction) $\to$
negligible. \textbf{PrivacyQA} maps the question to an OPP-115
category \citep{wilson2016opp115} whose severity tier reflects
the corresponding regulatory-fine band (Data Security and
Third-Party Sharing $\to$ critical/major; first-party
collection, user choice, access/edit $\to$ minor; descriptive
sections $\to$ negligible).

\section{Evaluation Protocol Details}
\label{sec:app-protocol}

This section expands on the body's \autoref{sec:protocol}.

\paragraph{Scoring cascade.}
Predictions are scored by the following cascade: option-letter
match for MCQ; yes/no extraction; numeric comparison at $5\%$
relative tolerance; exact text match after normalisation;
substring containment (min.\ $4$ chars); word-set overlap
(min.\ $3$ reference words).

\paragraph{Risk measures.}
For each (model, dataset) pair we compute
$\hat{p}{=}1{-}\mathrm{accuracy}$, the severity profile
$\hat{\bpi}$ from the empirical error distribution,
$\widehat{\ES}$ via \autoref{eq:expected_loss}, and the full
distribution of $S$ from $10^5$ Monte Carlo replications at
$n{=}10{,}000$ outputs per evaluation period (which yields
$\VaR_{0.95}$ and $\TVaR_{0.95}$). The Lundberg coefficient $R$
and reserve $u^\ast$ are computed at ruin tolerance
$\psi^\ast{=}0.01$; routing cost reduction is reported at
thresholds $d \in \{c_2, c_3\}$ with human review cost
$h{=}\$50$. All computations use the \seveval{} library
(\autoref{sec:app-library}).

\paragraph{Confidence intervals.}
Our benchmarks span three orders of magnitude in size
(financebench $n{=}150$, judgebert $n{=}1{,}483$, headqa
$n{=}12{,}751$, maud $n{=}39{,}231$), so per-dataset confidence
intervals on every reported quantity are essential to avoid
over-reading small-sample noise. For each (model, dataset) pair
we report:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Accuracy $\hat{p}$: Wilson score $95\%$ interval
    \citep{wilson1927ci}, which is well-behaved near
    $\hat{p}{=}0$ and $\hat{p}{=}1$ unlike the normal
    approximation.
  \item Severity profile $\hat{\bpi}$: Goodman simultaneous
    multinomial $95\%$ intervals on each $\hat{\pi}_k$, with the
    family-wise rate controlled via Bonferroni across $K{=}4$
    categories.
  \item $\widehat{\ES}$, $\VaR_{0.95}$, $\TVaR_{0.95}$:
    non-parametric percentile bootstrap with $B{=}1{,}000$
    resamples over the $10^5$-replication Monte Carlo loss
    vector, exposed through
    \texttt{severity\_eval.bootstrap\_ci}.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Pairwise comparisons and per-domain aggregation.}
For pairwise model comparisons within a dataset we use a paired
bootstrap on the per-instance loss vector
$L_i = \mathbb{1}\{\text{wrong}_i\} \cdot c(\text{severity}_i)$,
which controls for shared difficulty across instances. The
two-sample $z$-test on accuracy is reported only as a quick
sanity check; bootstrap intervals are the primary inferential
tool because they remain valid under the heavy-tailed cost
vector. Per-domain aggregates weight datasets by sample size to
avoid letting a small benchmark (financebench, $n{=}150$)
dominate a large one (maud, $n{=}39{,}231$) when the per-dataset
CIs differ by an order of magnitude.

\section{\seveval{} Library Details}
\label{sec:app-library}

\subsection{Core API}

The primary entry point is the \texttt{evaluate()} function:

\begin{small}
\begin{verbatim}
import severity_eval

report = severity_eval.evaluate(
    predictions=preds,
    references=refs,
    severity_annotations=labels,
    cost_levels=[100, 1_000, 10_000, 100_000],
    n_queries=10_000,
    n_sim=100_000,
    alpha=0.95,
)
\end{verbatim}
\end{small}

\noindent The returned \texttt{SeverityReport} object contains the
accuracy, error rate, severity profile $\hat{\bpi}$, expected loss,
VaR, TVaR, and the full vector of simulated losses. It supports
serialization to JSON, pandas DataFrames, and \LaTeX{} tables.

\subsection{Modules}

The actuarial pipeline is split across six analytical modules:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{compound\_loss}: Monte Carlo simulation of
    $S = \sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i$ under the binomial-categorical model.
  \item \textbf{risk\_measures}: Empirical VaR, TVaR, and bootstrap
    confidence intervals.
  \item \textbf{ruin}: Lundberg adjustment coefficient (via Brent's
    method), reserve requirements, and Monte Carlo ruin probability
    estimation.
  \item \textbf{routing}: Excess-of-loss routing analysis with
    configurable retention threshold and human review cost.
  \item \textbf{taxonomy}: Built-in severity taxonomies for finance,
    medicine, law and insurance, with a
    registration API for custom domains.
  \item \textbf{sensitivity}: Perturbation analysis of cost vectors
    (one coordinate at a time, $\pm$ directions) to assess robustness
    of model rankings.
\end{itemize}

Three auxiliary modules support the pipeline: \texttt{api} (the
\texttt{evaluate()} entry point and the \texttt{SeverityReport}
dataclass), \texttt{metric} (the HuggingFace \texttt{evaluate.Metric}
wrapper) and \texttt{validation} (input checks for severity profiles).
A \texttt{visualization} module provides the matplotlib plotting
helpers used by the figures in this paper.

\subsection{HuggingFace Integration}

The module \texttt{severity\_eval.metric} exposes
\texttt{compute\_severity\_metrics()}, a flat-dict wrapper around
the core \texttt{evaluate()} call, and \texttt{CompoundLossMetric},
a subclass of \texttt{evaluate.Metric} that can be instantiated
directly. Both paths return the same payload (accuracy, error rate,
severity profile, expected loss, VaR, TVaR), so the risk measures
can be reported alongside accuracy in a standard evaluation
pipeline. The metric is not published on the HuggingFace Hub as a
loadable module in this release.

\subsection{Reproducing the experiments}

The \texttt{experiments/run\_full\_pipeline.sh} script orchestrates
the full evaluation matrix: it evaluates the open-weight models
locally via vLLM with FP8 (or MXFP4-native for the gpt-oss family)
checkpoints, runs the analysis
(\texttt{experiments/analysis.py}) and regenerates all paper
figures and tables. Stages are idempotent and can be resumed with
the corresponding \texttt{--skip-*} flag.

\section{Robustness to Cost Calibration}
\label{sec:app-robustness}

Perturbing each $c_k$ by $\pm 20\%$ one coordinate at a time barely
moves the per-domain $\ES$ rankings: the minimum Spearman
correlation between the base and perturbed rankings is $0.93$ in
finance and $\geq 0.99$ in legal, insurance, and medical (mean
$\geq 0.97$ in all four). Moderate uncertainty in $\mathbf{c}$ thus
does not overturn rankings dominated by large differences in $\bpi$
and $p$. The full sensitivity table is generated by
\texttt{experiments/analysis.py} via the \texttt{--robustness} flag.
